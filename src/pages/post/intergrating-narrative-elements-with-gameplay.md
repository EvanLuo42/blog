The core gameplay mechanics, such as combat, exploration, and character progression, are designed to be enjoyable on their own. However, puzzle games prevent this by integrating narrative elements so deeply into the mechanics that the two become inseparable. One such example is "Gorogoa," which masterfully combines its puzzle mechanics with compelling narrative elements, creating an experience where story and gameplay are inextricably linked.

Similar to other puzzle games, "Gorogoa" also requires players to solve a series of puzzles to reveal the overall story. However, other puzzle games are usually designed in a linear narrative, meaning players can only progress to the next puzzle after solving the current one. Additionally, many puzzle games provide explicit feedback, such as a red cross on the screen, to indicate whether the player's current step is correct or incorrect. In contrast, Gorogoa doesn’t have these features. There is an overall puzzle, which involves making a final image using four blocks, alongside many smaller puzzles. For instance, if a player places one block in a particular spot and explores further, they will possibly find the two blocks can be connected together. This connection may not immediately expedite progress, but its significance becomes clear later. The gameplay of both jigsaw puzzles and "Gorogoa" revolves around collecting small successes and eventually combining them to create a final image.

The advantage of this gameplay is that, unlike other puzzle games, players have the freedom to experiment with the four images without being forced to follow a prescribed path. This non-linear approach means that each player can have a unique gameplay experience. However, players are also required to understand and remember the story or the theme of the images they encounter to piece together the final image. This mechanism seamlessly integrates narrative elements with the gameplay of jigsaw puzzles. Without grasping the theme, even if a player manages to connect some images by chance, achieving the final image remains a challenge. This kind of non-linear design is also present in "The Legend of Zelda: Breath of the Wild" and "Tears of the Kingdom." These two games also allow players to non-linearly achieve the tasks while still reaching the end. 

Nevertheless, using non-linear design to integrate narrative elements with gameplay has some shortcomings. The most notable shortcoming is that players don’t always know what to do next. This makes "Gorogoa" a very challenging game that requires players to have strong associative abilities. The same issue arises in the aforementioned Zelda games. Many players have complained that they don’t know what to do next until they watch others’ solutions. Therefore, to design a non-linear game, all actions should feel natural. By saying “natural”, I mean that players should be able to intuitively know what they need to do as soon as they encounter something, based on their real-life experiences.

In conclusion, while non-linear design effectively integrates narrative elements with gameplay and offers a unique experience, it also presents significant challenges. Games like "Gorogoa" and Zelda showcase this approach's potential, maximizing narrative importance and providing freedom and depth often missing in linear designs. However, the success of such designs depends on ensuring players can intuitively and naturally progress through the game. Balancing freedom and guidance is crucial for making non-linear narrative-driven games both accessible and engaging.
